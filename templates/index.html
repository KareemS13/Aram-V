What does MoM% mean?
Month-on-month (MoM%) tells you how much prices changed compared to the previous month. A reading of +0.5% means the average price of goods and services was 0.5% higher than last month. A negative reading means prices actually fell that month — which is rare. This is different from annual inflation (year-on-year), which compares to the same month last year.

Why does inflation spike sometimes?
Armenia's inflation is closely tied to global energy and food prices (imported through Russia and the EU), the AMD/USD exchange rate, and seasonal patterns (prices typically rise before the New Year and fall in summer). The large spike in early 2022 reflects the impact of the Russia–Ukraine war on energy and food import costs.

How is this forecast made?
The forecast combines two models: a SARIMA (statistical time-series model) that captures seasonal patterns, and a LightGBM (machine learning) model that incorporates exchange rates, oil prices, wheat prices, natural gas costs, and Armenia's trade flows. The two are blended based on their past accuracy — SARIMA currently leads. The further ahead the forecast, the wider the uncertainty band. Typical error: ±0.4 percentage points per month.

What are these factors?
Seasonality — prices follow predictable calendar patterns each year.
Energy — oil, natural gas, and electricity costs feed into transport and heating prices.
Exchange rate — when the AMD weakens against USD/EUR/RUB, imported goods cost more.
Food prices — wheat and food sub-index (cp01) directly affect the food basket.
Own momentum — high inflation tends to persist for a few months before fading.
Structural shocks — one-off events like COVID-19 or the 2022 Ukraine war.
